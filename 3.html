Solving Section — Motion (Speed • Distance • Time)

Step-by-step examples, interactive practice, and real-life Filipino problems to build your skills.

1. Introduction to Solving Motion Problems

Why do we solve speed-distance-time problems?

When you know how far and how long, you can find how fast something was moving. Solving these problems helps you understand everyday motion — like how long a jeepney ride will take, or how fast you must walk to reach school on time.

In simple terms: solving motion problems helps you predict time, distance, or speed in real situations. This means you can plan a trip, time a race, or check results from a simulation.

Real-Life Examples:
  • Walking to school — how long will it take if you walk 2 km?
  • Jeepney ride along a route — how fast did the jeepney move between stops?
  • Biking around the barangay — how far will you go in 15 minutes?

2. Formula Toolbox

Three simple formulas to remember

Speed = Distance ÷ Time — Use this if you know distance and time and need to find speed.
Distance = Speed × Time — Use this if you know speed and time and need distance.
Time = Distance ÷ Speed — Use this if you know distance and speed and need time.
Tip: Always check the units. If distance is in meters and time in seconds, the speed is m/s. If distance is in kilometers and time in hours, the speed is km/h.

3. Guided Examples (Step-by-step)

We solve problems slowly and clearly — each step explains why we do it.

Example 1 — Find Speed (Walking to School)

Problem: Maria walks 1.8 kilometers to school in 30 minutes. What is her speed in m/s?

Given: distance = 1.8 km, time = 30 min. Required: speed in m/s.
Step 1: Convert units so they match. 1.8 km = 1800 m. 30 minutes = 30 × 60 = 1800 seconds.
Step 2 (Formula): speed = distance ÷ time
Substitution: speed = 1800 m ÷ 1800 s
Solution: speed = 1 m/s
Mini diagram:
[School] ----------------- (1.8 km) ----------------- [Home]
Maria walks from Home to School in 30 min → Speed = 1 m/s

This means that Maria moves 1 meter every second.

Real-life connection: A steady walking pace (~1 m/s) helps you estimate how long it takes to reach school.

Example 2 — Find Distance (Jeepney on EDSA)

Problem: A jeepney travels at 40 km/h for 1.5 hours. How far does it go?

Given: speed = 40 km/h, time = 1.5 h. Required: distance in km.
Step 1 (Formula): distance = speed × time
Substitution: distance = 40 km/h × 1.5 h
Solution: distance = 60 km
Mini diagram:
[Start] --- 60 km --- [End]
Jeepney at 40 km/h for 1.5 h → 60 km

This means that the jeepney travels 60 kilometers in that time (example for long route planning).

Real-life connection: Use this to decide how early to leave if your trip is long.

Example 3 — Find Time (Student running on track)

Problem: A student runs 400 m around the track at 5 m/s. How long does it take?

Given: distance = 400 m, speed = 5 m/s. Required: time (s)
Step 1 (Formula): time = distance ÷ speed
Substitution: time = 400 ÷ 5
Solution: time = 80 s (1 minute 20 seconds)
Mini diagram:
[Start] ---------------- 400 m ---------------- [Finish]
At 5 m/s → time = 80 s

This means that the runner completes one lap in one minute and twenty seconds at that speed.

Real-life connection: Coaches use these calculations to measure performance.

4. Mini Challenge Problems (Try these)

Short problems — click "Show Solution" to reveal steps.

1) A tricycle goes 2 km in 10 minutes. What is its speed in km/h? [Show Solution ▼]
Step 1: Convert minutes to hours: 10 min = 10/60 = 1/6 h ≈ 0.1667 h.
Step 2 (Formula): speed = distance ÷ time = 2 km ÷ (1/6 h) = 2 × 6 = 12 km/h.
Answer: 12 km/h
This means the tricycle moves 12 kilometers in one hour at that pace.
2) A student cycles 9 km in 30 minutes. What is her speed in m/s? [Show Solution ▼]
Convert 9 km to meters: 9000 m. Convert 30 min to seconds: 1800 s.
speed = 9000 ÷ 1800 = 5 m/s.
Answer: 5 m/s
Good job — converting units first makes the math easy.
3) A car travels 150 km at 75 km/h. How long does it take? [Show Solution ▼]
time = distance ÷ speed = 150 ÷ 75 = 2 hours.
Answer: 2 h
This is a simple divide problem — no unit changes needed if both are in km and km/h.
4) Two students start at the same point. A walks at 1.5 m/s and B runs at 3 m/s. After 10 seconds how far apart are they? [Show Solution ▼]
Distance A = 1.5 × 10 = 15 m. Distance B = 3 × 10 = 30 m.
Difference = 30 − 15 = 15 m.
Answer: 15 m apart
This shows how different speeds lead to separation over time.
5) A jeepney covers 24 km in 30 minutes. What is its average speed in km/h? [Show Solution ▼]
30 minutes = 0.5 hours. speed = 24 ÷ 0.5 = 48 km/h.
Answer: 48 km/h
Average speed helps plan arrival times under normal conditions.

5. Application Problems (Think & Solve)

Longer problems — read carefully, draw a small picture, then solve.

A jeepney and a car start from the same point. The jeepney goes at 40 km/h and the car at 60 km/h. After 2 hours, how far ahead is the car? [Show Solution ▼]
Distance jeepney = 40 × 2 = 80 km. Distance car = 60 × 2 = 120 km.
Difference = 120 − 80 = 40 km.
Answer: The car is 40 km ahead.
Draw two lines showing distances to visualize who is ahead.
A cyclist travels 15 km at 10 km/h, then increases speed to 15 km/h and travels another 15 km. What is the average speed for the whole trip? [Show Solution ▼]
Time for first part = 15 ÷ 10 = 1.5 h. Time for second part = 15 ÷ 15 = 1.0 h. Total distance = 30 km. Total time = 2.5 h.
Average speed = total distance ÷ total time = 30 ÷ 2.5 = 12 km/h.
Answer: 12 km/h
Average speed is not the same as the speed in each part — compute with totals.
Two students walk toward each other from points 200 m apart. One walks at 1.5 m/s and the other at 1.0 m/s. How long until they meet? [Show Solution ▼]
Their closing speed = 1.5 + 1.0 = 2.5 m/s (because they move toward each other).
Time = distance ÷ closing speed = 200 ÷ 2.5 = 80 s.
Answer: They meet after 80 seconds (1 minute 20 seconds).
Relative motion adds speeds when moving toward each other.

8. Common Mistakes & How to Fix Them

  • ❌ Confusing speed with distance — Fix: Speed tells how fast; distance tells how far. Use formulas to separate them.
  • ❌ Forgetting to convert units — Fix: Always put everything in the same units (m & s or km & h) before calculating.
  • ❌ Mixing minutes and seconds — Fix: Convert minutes to seconds or hours to minutes first.
  • ❌ Thinking faster always means farther — Fix: Distance also depends on time. Fast for a short time may be less distance than slow for a long time.
Check Your Work: After solving, ask: Are the units correct? Does the answer make sense (is it too big or too small)?

9. Summary

Quick recap

  • Motion is change in position compared to a reference point.
  • Speed = distance ÷ time. Use matching units.
  • Distance = speed × time. Time = distance ÷ speed.
  • Draw a small diagram and convert units first — this helps avoid mistakes.
